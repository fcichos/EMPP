Spring Pendulum: When Two Motions Combine

In the previous lecture, we applied odeint to planetary motion - a system with radial and angular motion coupled by Newton‚Äôs gravity. The mathematics was elegant, and the orbits were stable and predictable.

Now let‚Äôs explore a seemingly simpler system that turns out to be much more complex: the spring pendulum.

Imagine hanging a weight from a spring. If you pull it down and release it, the spring bounces up and down. If you push it sideways, it swings like a pendulum. But what happens if you do both at the same time?

This seemingly simple question leads to one of the most fascinating systems in classical mechanics. Like planetary motion, it involves radial and angular coordinates. But unlike the stable ellipses we saw with planets, the spring pendulum can:

  • Exchange energy between modes
  • Show intricate patterns
  • Even become chaotic - deterministic yet unpredictable!

In this lecture, you‚Äôll:

  • Apply odeint to a system with coupled oscillation modes (building on planetary motion)
  • See how a different force law (spring instead of gravity) creates new behaviors
  • Understand energy exchange between radial and angular motion
  • Discover the route from regular to chaotic motion
  • Use phase space plots to distinguish order from chaos

The Physical Setup

The spring pendulum consists of: - A spring attached to a fixed point (spring constant \(k\)) - A mass \(m\) hanging from the spring - The spring‚Äôs natural (rest) length is \(L_0\)

Two Ways to Describe Motion

We need two coordinates to describe where the mass is at any time:

  1. \(L\): How much the spring is stretched (or compressed) beyond its natural length
    • \(L = 0\) means the spring is at its natural length
    • \(L > 0\) means stretched
    • \(L < 0\) means compressed
  2. \(\theta\): The angle from vertical
    • \(\theta = 0\) means hanging straight down
    • \(\theta > 0\) means swinging to the right
    • \(\theta < 0\) means swinging to the left

The total length of the pendulum at any moment is \(L_0 + L\).

Why These Coordinates?

We call \(L\) and \(\theta\) generalized coordinates. They‚Äôre natural choices because: - They directly measure the spring stretch and pendulum swing - They simplify the equations of motion - They‚Äôre called ‚Äúpolar coordinates‚Äù (radius and angle)

The Equations of Motion

Just like we learned in Week 5, we need to convert Newton‚Äôs second law (\(F = ma\)) into equations we can solve with odeint. For the spring pendulum, we get two coupled differential equations:

Radial Motion (Spring Direction)

\[\begin{equation} \ddot{L} = (L_0+L)\dot{\theta}^2-\frac{k}{m}L+g\cos(\theta) \end{equation}\]

Let‚Äôs understand each term:

  • \((L_0+L)\dot{\theta}^2\): The centrifugal effect - when the pendulum swings, the mass ‚Äúwants‚Äù to fly outward, stretching the spring
  • \(-\frac{k}{m}L\): The spring restoring force (Hooke‚Äôs law) - pulls back toward the natural length
  • \(g\cos(\theta)\): The radial component of gravity - when tilted, gravity has a component along the spring

Angular Motion (Swinging Direction)

\[\begin{equation} \ddot{\theta} = -\frac{1}{L_0+L}\left[g\sin(\theta)+2\dot{L}\dot{\theta}\right] \end{equation}\]

Understanding these terms:

  • \(g\sin(\theta)\): The gravitational torque - this is what makes a normal pendulum swing
  • \(2\dot{L}\dot{\theta}\): The coupling term - when the spring length changes, it affects the swing speed (like an ice skater spinning faster when pulling arms in!)
  • \(\frac{1}{L_0+L}\): The effect of changing pendulum length on rotation
The Key Insight: Coupling

These equations are coupled - they depend on each other! The spring stretch affects the swing, and the swing affects the spring stretch. This coupling creates the rich, complex behavior we‚Äôll explore.

Comparing to Planetary Motion

Notice the similarities to the previous lecture: - Planetary Motion: Radial force is \(-GM/r^2\) (Newton‚Äôs inverse square law) - Spring Pendulum: Radial force is \(-kL\) (Hooke‚Äôs linear law)

Same mathematical structure (centrifugal term + radial force + coupling), different force laws! The spring‚Äôs linear force creates very different behavior than gravity‚Äôs inverse square law.

Converting to First-Order System

Remember from Week 5: odeint needs first-order differential equations. We have second-order equations (involving \(\ddot{L}\) and \(\ddot{\theta}\)), so we need to convert them.

We introduce new variables for the velocities: - \(v = \dot{L}\) (rate of spring stretch) - \(\omega = \dot{\theta}\) (angular velocity)

This gives us four first-order equations:

\[\begin{align} \dot{L} &= v \\ \dot{v} &= (L_0+L)\omega^2-\frac{k}{m}L+g\cos(\theta) \\ \dot{\theta} &= \omega \\ \dot{\omega} &= -\frac{1}{L_0+L}\left[g\sin(\theta)+2v\omega\right] \end{align}\]

Our state vector is: \([L, v, \theta, \omega]\)

Understanding Phase Space

Phase space plots are a powerful tool for understanding dynamical systems. Instead of plotting position vs. time, we plot position vs. velocity.

Why Phase Space?

  • Each point represents the complete state of one degree of freedom
  • The trajectory shows how the system evolves
  • Regular motion creates closed curves
  • Chaotic motion creates strange patterns

Interpreting Phase Space Plots

  • Spiral inward: Damped oscillation (not in our case - we have no damping!)
  • Spiral outward: Growing amplitude
  • Tangled mess: Chaotic motion

The Road to Chaos

With certain combinations of parameters and initial conditions, the spring pendulum can exhibit chaotic motion - extremely sensitive to initial conditions and unpredictable over long times.

Signatures of Chaos

Chaotic motion shows: - Dense, irregular trajectories that fill space - No repeating patterns even over long times - Extreme sensitivity to initial conditions (change the starting point by 0.001 and get completely different behavior!) - Phase space ‚Äúattractors‚Äù with fractal structure

This is deterministic chaos - the equations are completely deterministic (no randomness!), but the behavior appears random.

Numerical Accuracy

The energy should be nearly constant. Small variations (typically < 0.1%) come from:

  • Numerical errors in odeint
  • Finite time step size

If you see large energy drift, try:

  • Increasing the number of time points N
  • Using tighter tolerances in odeint (see documentation)

Summary: What We‚Äôve Learned

üéì Key Takeaways

  1. Coupled Systems: The spring pendulum combines two degrees of freedom that influence each other

  2. ODE Solving Workflow (from Week 5):

    • Write equations of motion
    • Convert to first-order system
    • Define derivative function
    • Use odeint to solve

  3. Phase Space: A powerful visualization tool for understanding dynamics

    • Closed curves = periodic motion
    • Complex patterns = chaotic motion

  4. Energy Exchange: Energy can flow between different modes of motion

  5. Chaos: Even simple deterministic systems can show complex, unpredictable behavior

  6. Parameter Exploration: Small changes in parameters or initial conditions can dramatically change behavior
