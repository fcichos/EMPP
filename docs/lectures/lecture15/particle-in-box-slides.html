The Problem: Particle in a Box

A particle confined to a rectangular potential well:

\[V(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } |x| < d/2 \\ V_0 & \text{if } |x| \geq d/2 \end{cases}\]

Physical Intuition

Think of a guitar string:

  • Fixed at both ends (boundary conditions)
  • Only certain wavelengths fit ‚Üí discrete frequencies
  • Fundamental, first harmonic, second harmonic, ‚Ä¶

Same idea here:

  • Wavefunction confined to the box
  • Only certain wavelengths fit ‚Üí discrete energies
  • Ground state, first excited state, second excited state, ‚Ä¶

What Do We Expect?

For an infinite square well (analytical solution):

\[E_n = \frac{n^2 \pi^2 \hbar^2}{2mL^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\]

Key predictions:

Property Expectation
Energy spacing Discrete levels
Energy scaling \(E_n \propto n^2\)
Wavefunctions Standing waves with \(n-1\) nodes

Let‚Äôs verify this numerically!

Step 1: Define Parameters

# Physical constants
hbar = 1.055e-34  # Planck's constant / 2œÄ in J¬∑s
m_e = 9.109e-31   # electron mass in kg
V_0 = 20 * 1.602e-19  # barrier height: 20 eV in Joules

# Spatial grid
N = 1001          # number of grid points
d = 1e-9          # box width: 1 nm
L = 10e-9         # total domain: 10 nm

x = np.linspace(-L/2, L/2, N)
dx = x[1] - x[0]

Why these choices?

  • \(N = 1001\) points for good resolution
  • Domain larger than box to see decay outside
  • \(V_0 = 20\) eV gives ~7 bound states

Why V‚ÇÄ = 20 eV?

The ground state energy for a 1 nm box:

\[E_1 \approx \frac{\pi^2 \hbar^2}{2m_e d^2} \approx 0.38 \text{ eV}\]

Since \(E_n \propto n^2\):

Step 2: Build the Potential Matrix

# Create the potential energy vector
U_vec = np.zeros(N)
U_vec[np.abs(x) > d/2] = V_0  # V_0 outside, 0 inside

# Create diagonal sparse matrix
U = diags([U_vec], [0])

What this does:

  • np.abs(x) > d/2 selects points outside the box
  • Sets potential to \(V_0\) there, zero inside
  • diags creates a sparse diagonal matrix

The rectangular well: zero inside \(|x| < 0.5\) nm, 20 eV outside.

Step 3: Build the Kinetic Energy Matrix

The second derivative matrix times the prefactor:

\[\mathbf{T} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m \cdot dx^2} \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1 & 0 & \cdots \\ 1 & -2 & 1 & \cdots \\ \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots \end{pmatrix}\]

prefactor = -hbar**2 / (2 * m_e * dx**2)
T = prefactor * diags([-2., 1., 1.], [0, -1, 1], shape=(N, N))

Note: The prefactor includes the physical constants!

Step 4: Assemble the Hamiltonian

Simply add the two matrices:

\[\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{T} + \mathbf{V}\]

H = T + U

print(f"Matrix shape: {H.shape}")  # (1001, 1001)
print(f"Non-zero elements: {H.nnz}")  # ~3000 (tridiagonal + diagonal)

Sparse storage: Only ~3000 non-zero elements stored, not 1,002,001!

Step 5: Solve the Eigenvalue Problem

from scipy.sparse.linalg import eigsh

# Find the 20 smallest eigenvalues
n = 20
eigenvalues, eigenvectors = eigsh(H, k=n, which='SM')

Parameters explained:

Parameter Meaning
H The Hamiltonian matrix
k=n Number of eigenvalues to find
which='SM' Smallest Magnitude

Returns: eigenvalues (array) and eigenvectors (matrix, columns are eigenvectors)

Interpreting the Results

Observation 1: Discrete eigenvalues

Only certain energies are allowed ‚Äî the horizontal dashed lines.

Observation 2: Node counting

State Nodes (zeros inside box)
\(\Psi_1\) (ground) 0
\(\Psi_2\) 1
\(\Psi_3\) 2
\(\Psi_n\) \(n-1\)

General rule: Higher energy = more oscillations = more nodes

Interpreting the Results (continued)

Observation 3: Exponential decay outside

The wavefunctions don‚Äôt stop at the box edge ‚Äî they decay exponentially!

Mathematical reason: In the region where \(E < V(x)\):

\[\frac{d^2\Psi}{dx^2} = \frac{2m}{\hbar^2}(V - E)\Psi > 0\]

Positive curvature ‚Üí exponential behavior (not oscillatory)

This is called tunneling ‚Äî the particle has some probability of being found outside the classically allowed region!

# Second well: centered at +0.6 nm

Double Well: What to Expect

When wells are far apart:

  • Each well acts independently
  • Eigenvalues come in nearly-degenerate pairs
  • \(E_1 \approx E_2\), \(E_3 \approx E_4\), ‚Ä¶

When wells are close:

  • Wavefunctions can ‚Äútunnel‚Äù through the barrier
  • Eigenvalues split apart
  • Energy splitting increases with coupling strength

This is the physics behind chemical bonds!

The Power of This Approach

The same method works for any potential \(V(x)\):

  • Harmonic oscillator: \(V(x) = \frac{1}{2}kx^2\)
  • Morse potential: \(V(x) = D_e(1 - e^{-a(x-x_e)})^2\)
  • Periodic potential: Crystal lattices
  • Double well: Molecular bonds, ammonia inversion
  • Arbitrary shapes: Quantum dots, heterostructures

Just change U_vec! The rest of the code stays the same.

Beyond 1D

The same principles extend to higher dimensions:

\[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2\Psi + V(\vec{r})\Psi = E\Psi\]

The approach:

  • Discretize on a 2D or 3D grid
  • Build the Laplacian matrix (sparse!)
  • Add the potential
  • Solve the eigenvalue problem

The matrices get bigger, but the method is identical.

Questions?

Key equations:

Potential matrix: \(\mathbf{V} = \text{diag}(V(x_1), V(x_2), \ldots, V(x_N))\)

Kinetic matrix: \(\mathbf{T} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m \cdot dx^2}\begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1 \\ 1 & -2 & 1 \\ & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots \end{pmatrix}\)

Eigenvalue problem: \((\mathbf{T} + \mathbf{V})\vec{\Psi} = E\vec{\Psi}\)
