These same techniques will work for many other physics problems - anytime you have coupled motion in two dimensions. The mathematical framework is surprisingly general!

Historical Context: Newton‚Äôs Revolution

In 1687, Isaac Newton published the Principia Mathematica, arguably the most important physics book ever written. In it, he showed that:

  1. The same force that makes apples fall (gravity) also keeps planets in orbit
  2. All planetary motion could be explained by one simple equation: \(F = GMm/r^2\)
  3. Kepler‚Äôs empirical laws of planetary motion (discovered from observations) could be derived from Newton‚Äôs laws

This was revolutionary! It meant the same physics applies on Earth and in the heavens. Before Newton, these were thought to be fundamentally different realms.

Newton‚Äôs Insight

What made Newton‚Äôs work so powerful was that it was predictive, not just descriptive. Given a planet‚Äôs position and velocity at one moment, you could predict its entire future orbit. This is exactly what we‚Äôll do with odeint!

The Physical Setup

Imagine a planet orbiting the Sun:

  • The Sun is at the origin (we assume it‚Äôs much more massive than the planet, so it doesn‚Äôt move)
  • The planet has mass \(m\) and moves in a plane around the Sun
  • The only force is gravity: \(F = -\frac{GMm}{r^2}\) (pointing toward the Sun)

Where:

  • \(G\) is the gravitational constant
  • \(M\) is the mass of the Sun
  • \(m\) is the mass of the planet
  • \(r\) is the distance between them

Polar Coordinates: The Natural Choice

We‚Äôll use polar coordinates because they‚Äôre natural for this problem:

  1. \(r\): Distance from the Sun to the planet
  2. \(\theta\): Angular position (where the planet is in its orbit)

These are more natural than \((x, y)\) because:

  • Gravity acts in the radial direction
  • Angular momentum is naturally expressed in terms of \(\theta\)
  • The equations become simpler

The Equations of Motion

Using Newton‚Äôs second law in polar coordinates, we get two coupled differential equations:

Radial Motion

\[\begin{equation} \ddot{r} = r\dot{\theta}^2 - \frac{GM}{r^2} \end{equation}\]

Let‚Äôs understand each term:

  • \(r\dot{\theta}^2\): The centrifugal effect - the planet ‚Äúwants‚Äù to fly outward due to its orbital motion
  • \(-\frac{GM}{r^2}\): Newton‚Äôs gravitational force (per unit mass) - pulls the planet toward the Sun
    • Notice the \(1/r^2\) dependence - this is Newton‚Äôs inverse square law!

Angular Motion

\[\begin{equation} \ddot{\theta} = -\frac{2\dot{r}\dot{\theta}}{r} \end{equation}\]

This describes how the angular velocity changes:

  • \(2\dot{r}\dot{\theta}\): The coupling term - connects radial and angular motion
  • \(1/r\): As the planet gets closer to the Sun, it speeds up; farther away, it slows down
    • This is conservation of angular momentum in action!

The Analytical Solution: Conic Sections

Before we solve this numerically, it‚Äôs worth knowing what the analytical solution looks like. This is what Newton derived in the Principia!

The orbit equation is:

\[\begin{equation} r(\theta) = \frac{p}{1 + \varepsilon\cos(\theta)} \end{equation}\]

This is the equation of a conic section, where:

  • \(p = \frac{L^2}{GMm^2}\) depends on the angular momentum \(L\)
  • \(\varepsilon\) is the eccentricity, which depends on the energy \(E\):

\[\begin{equation} \varepsilon = \sqrt{1 + \frac{2EL^2}{m^3G^2M^2}} \end{equation}\]

What Do Different Eccentricities Mean?

  • \(\varepsilon = 0\): Perfect circle (e.g., an idealized orbit)
  • \(0 < \varepsilon < 1\): Ellipse (all planets in our solar system!)
    • Earth: \(\varepsilon \approx 0.017\) (nearly circular)
    • Mercury: \(\varepsilon \approx 0.206\) (noticeably elliptical)
  • \(\varepsilon = 1\): Parabola (escape trajectory - barely escapes)
  • \(\varepsilon > 1\): Hyperbola (escape trajectory - like some comets)
Kepler‚Äôs First Law

‚ÄúPlanets move in ellipses with the Sun at one focus.‚Äù

This is exactly what Newton‚Äôs equation predicts! We‚Äôll verify this numerically.

Converting to First-Order System

To use odeint, we need first-order differential equations. Let‚Äôs introduce:

  • \(v = \dot{r}\) (radial velocity)
  • \(\omega = \dot{\theta}\) (angular velocity)

Our state vector is: \([r, v, \theta, \omega]\)

This gives us four first-order equations:

\[\begin{align} \dot{r} &= v \\ \dot{v} &= r\omega^2 - \frac{GM}{r^2} \\ \dot{\theta} &= \omega \\ \dot{\omega} &= -\frac{2v\omega}{r} \end{align}\]

Setting Up: Units Matter!

Before simulating, we need to choose units carefully. Let‚Äôs use astronomical units:

  • Distance: AU (1 AU = Earth-Sun distance ‚âà 150 million km)
  • Time: years
  • Mass: Solar masses (\(M_{\odot}\))

In these units, something magical happens: \(G = 4\pi^2\)!

Why \(G = 4\pi^2\)?

From Kepler‚Äôs third law, for a circular orbit: \[T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM}r^3\]

For Earth (\(r = 1\) AU, \(T = 1\) year, \(M = 1 M_{\odot}\)): \[1 = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM} \cdot 1\] \[GM = 4\pi^2\]

Since we set \(M = 1\), this means \(G = 4\pi^2\) in our units!

Why Do We Need a Phase Shift?

You might wonder why we needed to calculate theta_0_analytical - why don‚Äôt the orbits match automatically?

The issue: The analytical orbit formula \(r(\theta) = \frac{p}{1 + \varepsilon\cos(\theta)}\) has a built-in assumption: perihelion (closest point to the Sun) is at angle Œ∏ = 0.

The problem: Our numerical simulation starts at:

  • Position: r = 1.0 AU, Œ∏ = 0
  • Velocities: v_r = 0, œâ = 1.8 rad/yr

This starting point is NOT at perihelion! The planet is somewhere else in its orbit when Œ∏ = 0.

The solution: We calculate where the planet actually starts in the analytical orbit frame by solving: \[r_0 = \frac{p}{1 + \varepsilon\cos(\theta_0)} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \theta_0 = \arccos\left(\frac{p/r_0 - 1}{\varepsilon}\right)\]

This theta_0_analytical tells us how much to rotate the analytical orbit so it matches the numerical simulation‚Äôs starting point.

Physical interpretation: Both orbits are the same ellipse, just rotated relative to each other! The shape, size, and period are identical - only the orientation in space differs.

Perfect Agreement!

After accounting for the phase shift, the numerical and analytical solutions should overlap almost perfectly. This confirms: 1. Our numerical method is accurate 2. Newton‚Äôs equations correctly predict orbits 3. odeint is a reliable tool for solving ODEs

Any small remaining differences come from numerical rounding errors.

Summary: What We‚Äôve Learned

üéì Key Takeaways

  1. Universal Physics: The same mathematical framework can describe many different physical systems - you just change the force law!

  2. Newton‚Äôs Triumph: Newton‚Äôs law of gravitation (\(F = GMm/r^2\)) perfectly explains planetary orbits

  3. Kepler‚Äôs Laws Verified:

    • First Law: Planets move in ellipses ‚úì
    • Second Law: Angular momentum is conserved ‚úì
    • Third Law: \(T^2 \propto a^3\) ‚úì

  4. Computational Methods: We used the same odeint workflow:

    • Define equations of motion
    • Convert to first-order system
    • Solve numerically
    • Verify with analytical solutions

  5. Orbit Types: Different energies create different trajectories:

    • E < 0: Bound elliptical orbits
    • E = 0: Parabolic escape
    • E > 0: Hyperbolic escape

  6. Conservation Laws: Energy and angular momentum are conserved, providing powerful checks on our numerical solutions

Historical Impact: Why This Matters

Newton‚Äôs solution to planetary motion wasn‚Äôt just a scientific achievement - it changed how humanity views the universe:

  1. Predictive Power: Astronomers could predict planetary positions centuries in advance

  2. Discovery of Neptune: In 1846, Neptune was discovered because Uranus‚Äôs orbit didn‚Äôt match predictions - something must be pulling on it! Adams and Le Verrier calculated where Neptune should be, and astronomers found it there.

  3. Space Age: Every satellite, probe, and spacecraft uses Newton‚Äôs equations to calculate trajectories

  4. Philosophical Impact: The universe operates by mathematical laws that humans can understand and use

From Newton to Einstein

Newton‚Äôs equations work perfectly for planetary motion‚Ä¶ except for tiny deviations in Mercury‚Äôs orbit. These tiny differences led Einstein to develop General Relativity in 1915. Even Newton‚Äôs ‚Äúwrong‚Äù theory is spectacularly accurate!

The spring pendulum consists of a mass attached to a spring that can both stretch and swing like a pendulum. What makes it particularly interesting is that it uses the same mathematical framework we‚Äôve developed here - we‚Äôll still be solving coupled first-order ODEs using scipy.integrate.solve_ivp - but the force law will be different, combining both elastic restoring forces and gravitational forces.

The spring pendulum is special because it shows how two different modes of motion - radial oscillation and angular swing - can couple together in surprising ways. Unlike the planetary motion we‚Äôve studied today, where the orbits are stable and predictable, the spring pendulum can exhibit rich dynamical behavior. Depending on the initial conditions and system parameters, you might see regular periodic motion, quasi-periodic behavior where the system never quite repeats itself, or even deterministic chaos where tiny changes in initial conditions lead to wildly different outcomes. This will be your first glimpse into the fascinating world of nonlinear dynamics!
