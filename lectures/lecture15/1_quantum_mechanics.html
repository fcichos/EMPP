Quantum Mechanics

Introduction: A Differential Equation Problem

Today we will solve a famous differential equation from physics: the Schr√∂dinger equation. Don‚Äôt worry‚Äîyou don‚Äôt need to understand quantum mechanics to follow this lecture! We will treat the Schr√∂dinger equation simply as what it is: a second-order differential equation that we can convert into a matrix eigenvalue problem.

The approach we‚Äôll use connects several things we‚Äôve learned:

  1. Finite differences to approximate derivatives (from earlier lectures)
  2. Matrix formulation of differential equations
  3. Eigenvalue problems of the form \(A\vec{x} = \lambda \vec{x}\) (from linear algebra)

The physical interpretation is interesting but optional for our purposes. What matters is that we can apply our numerical tools to solve this equation.

The Equation We Want to Solve

The stationary Schr√∂dinger equation in one dimension is:

\[ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2 \Psi(x)}{dx^2} + V(x)\Psi(x) = E\Psi(x) \]

Let‚Äôs identify what each term means mathematically:

Symbol Mathematical Role Physical Meaning (optional)
\(\Psi(x)\) Unknown function we want to find Wave function
\(\frac{d^2 \Psi}{dx^2}\) Second derivative of \(\Psi\) Related to kinetic energy
\(V(x)\) A given function (the ‚Äúpotential‚Äù) Potential energy
\(E\) Unknown constant (eigenvalue) Total energy
\(\hbar, m\) Physical constants Planck‚Äôs constant, mass
What kind of problem is this?

This is an eigenvalue problem! We‚Äôre looking for:

  • Eigenfunctions \(\Psi(x)\): special functions that, when processed through the differential operator, return a constant times themselves
  • Eigenvalues \(E\): the constants associated with each eigenfunction

This is exactly like \(A\vec{x} = \lambda\vec{x}\) from linear algebra, but in function space!

From Differential Equation to Matrix Problem

Our strategy is to convert this continuous differential equation into a discrete matrix equation that we can solve with standard linear algebra tools.

Step 1: Discretize the Domain

Instead of considering \(\Psi(x)\) for all real \(x\), we evaluate it only at discrete points:

\[x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots, x_N\]

with spacing \(\delta x\). Our unknown function \(\Psi(x)\) becomes a vector:

\[\vec{\Psi} = \begin{bmatrix} \Psi(x_1) \\ \Psi(x_2) \\ \vdots \\ \Psi(x_N) \end{bmatrix}\]

Step 2: Discretize the Second Derivative

We‚Äôve seen before that the second derivative can be approximated by finite differences:

\[\frac{d^2\Psi}{dx^2}\bigg|_{x_i} \approx \frac{\Psi(x_{i+1}) - 2\Psi(x_i) + \Psi(x_{i-1})}{\delta x^2}\]

This operation can be written as a matrix multiplication:

\[\frac{d^2}{dx^2}\vec{\Psi} \approx \frac{1}{\delta x^2} \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0\\ 1 & -2 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0\\ 0 & 1 & -2 & 1 & \cdots & 0\\ \vdots & & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots\\ 0 & \cdots & 0 & 1 & -2 & 1\\ 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 1 & -2\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \Psi(x_{1})\\ \Psi(x_{2})\\ \Psi(x_{3})\\ \vdots\\ \Psi(x_{N-1})\\ \Psi(x_{N}) \end{bmatrix}\]

This is a tridiagonal matrix with \(-2\) on the diagonal and \(1\) on the off-diagonals.

Boundary Conditions

The matrix above implicitly assumes \(\Psi(x_0) = 0\) and \(\Psi(x_{N+1}) = 0\) (the function vanishes at the boundaries). These are called Dirichlet boundary conditions.

Step 3: Discretize the Potential Term

The potential energy term \(V(x)\Psi(x)\) is simpler‚Äîit‚Äôs just multiplication by a function at each point. In matrix form, this becomes a diagonal matrix:

\[V(x)\vec{\Psi} = \begin{bmatrix} V(x_{1}) & 0 & \cdots & 0\\ 0 & V(x_{2}) & \cdots & 0\\ \vdots & & \ddots & \vdots\\ 0 & \cdots & 0 & V(x_{N})\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \Psi(x_{1})\\ \Psi(x_{2})\\ \vdots\\ \Psi(x_{N}) \end{bmatrix}\]

Step 4: Assemble the Full Matrix

Combining both terms, our differential equation becomes:

\[\underbrace{\left(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\mathbf{T} + \mathbf{V}\right)}_{\mathbf{H}}\vec{\Psi} = E\vec{\Psi}\]

where:

  • \(\mathbf{T}\) is the tridiagonal second-derivative matrix
  • \(\mathbf{V}\) is the diagonal potential matrix
  • \(\mathbf{H} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\mathbf{T} + \mathbf{V}\) is called the Hamiltonian matrix

This is now a standard matrix eigenvalue problem: \(\mathbf{H}\vec{\Psi} = E\vec{\Psi}\)

Summary: The Recipe

To solve the Schr√∂dinger equation numerically:

  1. Choose a spatial grid \(x_1, \ldots, x_N\) with spacing \(\delta x\)
  2. Build the kinetic energy matrix \(\mathbf{T}\) (tridiagonal: \(-2\) diagonal, \(1\) off-diagonal)
  3. Build the potential energy matrix \(\mathbf{V}\) (diagonal with values \(V(x_i)\))
  4. Combine into \(\mathbf{H} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\mathbf{T} + \mathbf{V}\)
  5. Solve the eigenvalue problem \(\mathbf{H}\vec{\Psi} = E\vec{\Psi}\) using scipy.sparse.linalg.eigsh

The eigenvalues \(E\) are the allowed energies, and the eigenvectors \(\vec{\Psi}\) are the corresponding wave functions.

Key Insight

We transformed a continuous differential equation into a discrete matrix eigenvalue problem. The mathematics is the same as finding eigenvalues of any matrix‚Äîscipy does the heavy lifting!

In the next section, we‚Äôll apply this recipe to a concrete example: a particle trapped in a ‚Äúbox‚Äù (a potential well).

Exercise: Building the Second Derivative Matrix

Before we move on, let‚Äôs practice building the matrix representation of the second derivative operator.

Task: Create a function that builds the tridiagonal matrix for the second derivative and test it on a simple function \(f(x) = x^2\), for which we know the analytical second derivative is \(f''(x) = 2\).

Hint

Use scipy.sparse.diags() to build the tridiagonal matrix efficiently:

from scipy.sparse import diags
D2 = diags([-2, 1, 1], [0, -1, 1], shape=(N, N)) / dx**2
  • First argument [-2, 1, 1]: the values on each diagonal
  • Second argument [0, -1, 1]: the positions (0 = main, -1 = below, +1 = above)
  • shape=(N, N): the matrix dimensions

To apply a sparse matrix to a vector, use D2 @ f (the result is a dense array).
