Seminar: Projectile Motion with Air Resistance

Applying ODE Methods to Real Physics

Introduction

In today‚Äôs seminar, we will apply the numerical methods from the lecture to solve a classic physics problem: projectile motion with air resistance.

This problem is perfect for practicing because:

  • You know the physics from your mechanics course
  • Without air resistance, you can verify your code against the analytical solution
  • With air resistance, there is no analytical solution ‚Äî you need numerical methods!

What You Will Learn

  1. How to translate physics equations into code
  2. How to handle 2D motion with coupled ODEs
  3. How to compare different physical models
  4. How to analyze and visualize results

The Physics

A projectile of mass \(m\) experiences two forces:

  1. Gravity: \(\vec{F}_g = -mg\hat{y}\)
  2. Air resistance (drag): \(\vec{F}_d = -b\vec{v}\) (linear) or \(\vec{F}_d = -c|\vec{v}|\vec{v}\) (quadratic)

From Newton‚Äôs second law: \(m\vec{a} = \vec{F}_g + \vec{F}_d\)

Part 1: Projectile Without Air Resistance (Warmup)

Before adding complexity, let‚Äôs verify our numerical method works by solving a problem we can solve analytically.

The Equations

Without drag, the equations of motion are:

\[\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = 0 \qquad \frac{d^2y}{dt^2} = -g\]

Converting to first-order ODEs (state vector: \([x, y, v_x, v_y]\)):

\[\frac{dx}{dt} = v_x \qquad \frac{dv_x}{dt} = 0\] \[\frac{dy}{dt} = v_y \qquad \frac{dv_y}{dt} = -g\]

Analytical Solution (for comparison)

\[x(t) = x_0 + v_{x0} t\] \[y(t) = y_0 + v_{y0} t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\]

Part 2: Adding Linear Air Resistance

Now let‚Äôs add physics that cannot be solved analytically in 2D!

Linear Drag (Stokes Drag)

For slow-moving objects or small particles, air resistance is proportional to velocity:

\[\vec{F}_d = -b\vec{v}\]

where \(b\) is a drag coefficient. The equations of motion become:

\[m\frac{dv_x}{dt} = -bv_x \qquad m\frac{dv_y}{dt} = -mg - bv_y\]

We define the drag parameter \(\gamma = b/m\) (units: 1/s), so:

\[\frac{dv_x}{dt} = -\gamma v_x \qquad \frac{dv_y}{dt} = -g - \gamma v_y\]

  • \(a_y = -g - \gamma \cdot v_y\) (was just \(-g\))

Part 3: Quadratic Air Resistance (Realistic)

For everyday objects moving through air at reasonable speeds, the drag is actually proportional to velocity squared:

\[\vec{F}_d = -\frac{1}{2}\rho C_d A |\vec{v}| \vec{v}\]

where:

  • \(\rho\) = air density (‚âà 1.2 kg/m¬≥)
  • \(C_d\) = drag coefficient (‚âà 0.47 for a sphere)
  • \(A\) = cross-sectional area

We simplify by defining \(k = \frac{\rho C_d A}{2m}\), giving:

\[\frac{dv_x}{dt} = -k \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2} \cdot v_x\] \[\frac{dv_y}{dt} = -g - k \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2} \cdot v_y\]

Part 4: Analysis ‚Äî Finding the Optimal Angle

A classic question: What launch angle gives maximum range?

  • Without drag: The answer is 45¬∞ (you can prove this analytically)
  • With drag: The optimal angle is less than 45¬∞ ‚Äî but by how much?
